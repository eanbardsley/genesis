I put my paper The Mathematical Nature of Life up for discussion at academia.com. I found
since it is inter-disciplinary, in that it is Biology, Mathematics, Chemistry, Computer Science,
and Artificial Intelligence, that I had to explain things from one field to the other at times. I am
not formally trained in biology, but am in mathematics, chemistry, and computer science. I have
had to educate myself a bit in biology to write this paper. I would like to present here the
discussions that resulted from this paper, so those who are not cross-disciplinary in these fields
can better understand it. The abstract for it is:
An eﬀort is made to show that life, whether artiﬁcial or biological, does not just follow the laws
of chemistry, but is mathematical in Nature. Since we understand biological life in terms of
artiﬁcial intelligence (AI) and AI in terms of biological life we must ﬁrst ﬁnd the connection
between the two. To do this we must do it in terms of measurable numbers, like molar mass,
density, and atomic radius, if it is to be that it is mathematical in nature.
I ﬁrst show that the most basic structure of much of life, the skeleton, is algebraic with respect
to AI in molar mass and density. I then create from this certain mathematical operators which
yield -30 (negative 30) with each successive application to other substances, like water, air and
earth.
We then go on to the interesting case of what results in the AI elements because of boron, then
to the amino acids. I have not ﬁgured out an approach to DNA yet, because I think it stands
outside of all this encoding the mathematics used on the elements to make life."

Ian Beardsley writes:
Ruud, thanks for reading it over. It is really very chemistry oriented. Let me explain it like this:
Let's say we have the most simple organic compound, if something is organic it is by definition a
hydrocarbon, which merely means it is composed of at least the element hydrogen combined
with carbon. It probably has some oxygen atoms and some nitrogen atoms. The most simple
organic compound would have one of each of these and that is isocyanic acid which is written
HNCO which means it combines as H-N=C=O. By the laws of chemistry it combines like this
because hydrogen without its electron is H+, the single plus meaning it has a single bond.
Nitrogen is N3- meaning it has three negative electrons for bonding. We see in the above
diagram that one of those bind with single hydrogen bond H, and two of those bond with single
carbon (C). As we progress next to carbon it has a charge of C4- meaning it has four electrons for
bonding. We see it shares two with the nitrogen and two with oxygen and this is nice because the
oxygen is O2- meaning it needs two and it gets those from carbon. These are the laws of
chemistry that determine the way H, N, C, and O bond to make isocyanic acid. What I am doing
is looking at the molar mass of HNCO, because these are the most abundant elements in
biological life. When I say molar mass I mean the mass of a specified numbers of molecules of
HNCO (a mole being Avogadro's number of atoms). I then look for a mathematical law relating
these biological elements to the artificial intelligence elements like phosphorus, boron, and
silicon, where phosphorus and boron are doping agents that make silicon semiconduct so you
can makes diodes and transistors which can in turn be used to make the logic circuits that make
a computer work. I seek to find that the mathematical law that relates the molar masses of these
artificial intelligence elements to these biological life elements. We find that the connection is
through the golden ratio and that the mathematical laws for the relationship are the arithmetic
mean which is (a+b)/2 , the harmonic mean, and the geometric mean. This is at the beginning
of the paper, but I go on to find more mathematical laws for these elements in the different roles
they play in different components of life, like amino acids, or bone made of collagen and
hydroxyapatite. That what this paper strives to do is to find if life is not just a chemical construct
like I explained above, but a mathematical one.

The fibonacci series is formed by taking zero and adding one to it to get,
0,1,1,
then adding the one to the one to get
0,1,1,2
then adding the two to the one to get
0,1,1,2,3,...
and so on. The ratios between adjacent terms are closer and closer approximations to the golden
ratio. That is we have
0,1,1,2,3,5,8,…
2/1=2 ~ golden ratio = 1.618
3/2=1.5 ~ golden ratio = 1.618
5/3=1.667
...

We see successive ratios between adjacent terms in the Fibonacci sequence get closer and closer
to the golden ratio. At infinity it converges on the golden ratio. The golden ratio and the
Fibonacci sequence are different expressions of the same thing. If you have the golden ratio in
nature, you have the Fibonacci sequence, if you have the Fibonacci sequence in nature, you have
the golden ratio.

Ian Beardsley wrote:
phi can have many functions. It is often associated with closest packing. Because it is the most
irrational number. This is because for instance in Nature, a leaf rotates around the stem of a
plant by the fraction of the golden ratio, by the golden angle, so that each successive leaf is not
directly below another so as to provide sunlight to each leaf. One leaf cannot be directly below
another if rotated by the golden angle because the golden ratio cannot be written as a ratio
between to whole numbers. That is if rotated 1/4 of the way around the stem to the next leaf,
after four leaves, one leaf would be directly below the first.
